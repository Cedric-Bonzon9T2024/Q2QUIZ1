The baro't saya, the national costume of the Philippines, has its origins in the pre-colonial period and was influenced by Spanish colonization The most popular clothing that has been a big part of the Filipino identity, are the Barong Tagalog for men and the Baro’t Saya for women. The Filipino clothing style was influenced by the Spaniards who colonized the Philippines from 1565 to 1898. After the Spanish era, it has continuously evolved and withstood the test of time.
Kegal T'boli: Kegal T'boli is the traditional clothing of the T'boli tribe, known for its vibrant colors and intricate beadwork. The ensemble includes a blouse called Kegal Bentilas, a skirt called Lewek tedeyung, beaded belts known as hilet lemimet, a necklace called bekelew, and a headdress called sewat. Kegal Bensewit (decorated with shell discs) is the most expensive Tboli blouse. Kegal Lemimet is a blouse with white beads and originally used when you play musical instruments. The white beads sparks once under sunlight that is believed to interact with the spirit of nature. Kegal Bentilas, blouse decorated with sequins that is also seen among Blaan women in Sarangani Province. Kegal Kenibang, a blouse decorated with applique’. Kegal Nesif is a cross-stitched/embroidered blouse composed of geometrical designs inspired from the nature like flowers, sun, leaf, human and crucifix. It takes at least 2months to finish this by hand embroidery. Kegal Betaku is a plain black blouse with red line of the shoulder and elbow for a boundary between the witch and the goddess to protect human baby. Kegal Tnalak is the cloth made of hand-woven abaca cloth. It depends on the kind of Tnalak design you choose to wear.
Igorot costume is very simple. The men wear long strips of handwoven loin cloth called “wanes bahag”. The woman wear a kind of wrap-around skirt called “lufid or tapis”. This attire is used by the tribes in Mountain Province of The Cordillera ranges, called Igorots. The traditional clothing of the Igorot people of the Philippines is colorful, handwoven, and includes: • Skirts: Known as wanes • Blankets: Known as badjao • Accessories: Beaded necklaces, earrings, and brass or bead headgear The Igorot are an ethnic group in the mountains of northern Luzon, Philippines. They have traditionally maintained their own religion and way of life, and most live in the region's higher elevations.
The Moro People are the indigenous Muslim inhabitants mostly located in southwestern Mindanao and the Sulu Archipelago. The Moro People are known for their skilled weavers and tailors. Moro Women wear a traditional clothing called a “Malong” which is a colorful woven cloth wrapped around the body. Both men and women of the Moro People wear a large length of fabric wrapped around their waist called a “Patadyong”. The Moro-moro is a performing art in the Philippines that features costumes and is based on the Spanish colonial-era battles between Christians and Moors. The costumes and other aspects of the Moro-moro originated from a variety of sources, including: Spanish priests- Spanish priests created the Moro-moro as a way to spread Christianity and eliminate indigenous theatrical forms. Indigenous traditions The Moro-moro was influenced by the indigenous awit and corrido traditions of Philippine native cultures. Islamic armors- Moro armors were often made of brass or a combination of brass mail and horn plates from the Carabao, a Filipino subspecies of the water buffalo. The armors opened down the front like a shirt, making them easy to take on and off. The Moro-moro is still performed today at church festivals in villages, and is a major social and religious event
The Tuwali Ifugao also known as Kiangan, Ifugao or Gilipanes are located in the municipality of Kiangan at the southern end of the Ifugao Province, they have 15 localities and 3 dialects. The male traditional clothing of the Ifugaos are called “Wanno” or G-string. Depending on the occasion or the man’s social status, there are six different types of wanno to be worn.The female traditional clothing of the Ifugaos is called “Tapis” otherwise known as a wraparound skirt. There are five different types of skirts. The Ifugao people inhabit the mountainous Cordillera region in northern Luzon in the Philippines. They have a rich culture centered around agriculture. Their traditional clothing includes loincloths for men and wraparound skirts for women. Important aspects of their material culture include musical instruments like gangsa gongs, carved wooden bulul figures, imbule stews, fermented rice wine, and native houses built on posts. Their non-material culture includes a traditional healing practice called mumbaki, engagement customs like the moma agreement, expensive wedding and funeral ceremonies, and a three-day feasting ritual called bogwah instead of mourning.
